I + M SYLLABLES

Back to CONTENT

Korean syllables always consist of two or three letters (the third is optional) combined in one of the special orders.

The first letter is always a consonant (initial). The second one is always a vowel (medial). The last one is a consonant (or a consonant cluster) and is called patchim (final).

If we take a horizontal vowel when building up a syllable, we combine letters like this

ㅅ
+
ㅗ

and get 소 (so).


If the vowel is vertical we combine them this way

ㄴ + ㅐ

and get 내 (nae).

Actually, there are eight ways to write a syllable, but they are almost the same and use simple logic.

We have to keep I + M composition even if a syllable represents only a vowel sound like in 우 (u), 예 (ye). In this case we always put the letter ㅇ at the beginning of a syllable. ㅇ has no sound and isn't pronounced when used as an initial consonant. So 아 or 유 are pronounced the same way as just ㅏ or ㅠ etc. The rule works for all of the vowels.

Letters in syllables are always read from left to right then from top to bottom.

I + M + F SYLLABLES

Back to CONTENT

There are more complex syllables. Those are of I + M + F composition. They are over 10 000 and visualizing them in a table doesn't make sense. For example: 쌙 (ssaen), 툔 (tyon), 응 (eung), 뿄 (ppyot), 앹 (aet).

Only a consonant or a consonant cluster can be used as a final letter. It's called patchim.

Their sound may differ from their "initial" sound.

If we can read syllables with final consonants then we almost can read words. The application generates this kind of syllables randomly in an endless loop.


Note, for example, that the letter ㅇ now has ng ([ŋ]) sound instead of none, and the letter ㅅ sounds like t instead of s.

Some of them sound differently depending on a situation (i.g., ㄺ can be pronounced as both k and l).

There are rules about how a patchim is read depending on letters it is between. Learning those rules until you can read words of two or more syllables without mistakes will take time and practice.

In my opinion, the best result is achieved by using workbooks and writing exercises from books. I would recommend exactly this way.

When building up an I + M + F syllable we follow the same rules as the previous ones.

If the vowel is horizontal we combine letters like

ㄱ
+
ㅡ
+
ㄹ

and get 글 (geul).


If it's a vertical one we do

ㅎ+ㅏ
+
ㄴ

and get 한 (han).


IF YOU ARE A NOVICE

Back to CONTENT

You may have questions like:

- why do they write the sound of ㄱ like "g" but pronounce it "k"?

- what's the difference between ㄱ, ㅋ and ㄲ (aren't they pronounced the same way)?

- what's the difference between ㅗ and ㅜ?

- what's the difference between ㅐ and ㅔ?

- why is "m" often sounds more like "b"?

You will get used to this and understand the things in time as you read, watch and listen to different sources of information.
